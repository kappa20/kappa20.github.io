Gerund & Infinitive

Verb + -ing (Gerund)

These verbs are always followed by the gerund (-ing form):

admitappreciate avoidcan't help delaydeny detestdiscuss dislikeenjoy escapeface fancyfeel like finishgive up involvekeep (on) mentionmind misspostpone practiseput off resistrisk suggestunderstand

✓ I've finished writing my essay.  ✓ She avoided answering the question.

Watch out! After a preposition, always use -ing: I'm interested in hearing more.

Verb + Full Infinitive (to + verb)

These verbs are followed by the full infinitive:

ableafford agreeappear arrangeask attemptbeg choosedecide encourageexpect failhappen helphope manageoffer planpleased preparepretend promiserefuse seemtend wantwish would like

✓ She refused to answer.  ✓ I managed to escape.

Verb + Bare Infinitive (no "to")

feelhear letmake noticesee watch

✓ She made me stand in the corner.  ✓ I heard her sing.

Watch out! In passive: I was made to stand in the corner.

Verbs with CHANGE in meaning

Verb+ -ing+ infinitive
rememberrecall a past event: I remember seeing him.not forget to do sth: Remember to lock the door.
forgetrecall a past event: I'll never forget meeting her.fail to remember: I forgot to call him.
stopcease an activity: He stopped smoking.pause to do sth else: He stopped to buy coffee.
tryexperiment: Try adding salt.attempt: Try to be on time.
regretfeel sorry about past: I regret saying that.sorry to say (formal): I regret to inform you…
meaninvolve: This means working late.intend: I mean to finish today.
go oncontinue same thing: She went on talking.move to next thing: She went on to explain.

Prefer / Would rather / Had better

FormUseExample
prefer + -ing + to + -inggeneral preferenceI prefer reading to watching TV.
would prefer + inf + rather than + infspecific preferenceI'd prefer to stay rather than go.
would rather + bare infgeneral/specific preferenceI'd rather stay at home.
would rather + sb + past simplepreference about sb elseI'd rather you didn't smoke here.
had better + bare infadvice / warningYou'd better see a doctor.
Watch out! We say I prefer not to (not "I don't prefer").

Infinitives of Purpose

Use these to express WHY someone does something:

  • to + infinitive: I went there to see him.
  • in order to + inf: I studied in order to pass.
  • so as to + inf: She left early so as to avoid traffic.
  • in order not to / so as not to (negative): I whispered so as not to wake them.

Writing Skills

Essay Structure

ParagraphContent
Intro (§1)State the situation / topic. Give your general stance or introduce the debate.
Arguments for (§2)1st reason + development. 2nd reason + development. Use: Firstly… Secondly…
Arguments against (§3)Counter-arguments. Use: On the other hand… However… A further point is…
Your view (§4)Your opinion + solution/suggestion.
Conclusion (§5)Summary + final opinion. Use: In conclusion… To sum up…

Formal Letter / Job Application

  • Opening: I am writing in response to your advertisement in [paper] of [date] for [post].
  • Interest: I am very interested in the advertised post and believe I have the right experience…
  • Experience: Describe relevant qualifications and experience concretely.
  • Qualities: I appreciate that the role requires… and I know I can bring…
  • References: References can be obtained from…
  • Closing: I hope you will give my application favourable consideration and look forward to hearing from you.
  • Sign-off: Yours sincerely (named recipient) / Yours faithfully (Dear Sir/Madam)

Report / Scientific Report Structure

  • Give a proper title.
  • Divide into sections with headings.
  • State aim at the beginning: The aim/purpose of this report is to…
  • Avoid repeating the exact words of the question.

Making Recommendations (formal → informal)

  • I recommend that the council should set up…
  • I would recommend setting up…
  • I suggest buying more equipment…
  • It would be a good idea for the council to provide…
  • Why not + bare infinitive: Why not phone one of your friends?
  • How about + -ing: How about meeting up after work?
  • Let's + bare infinitive: Let's ask everyone's opinion.

Model Essay — Annotated Example How to structure a short essay.docx

Question: "All young people should continue at school until at least the age of 18. Do you agree?"

§1 — Introduction: situation + stance

Although in my country compulsory education finishes at the age of 16, I believe that young people should continue in education at least till they are 18.

§2 — Arguments FOR (2 reasons)

There are two good reasons for encouraging young people to stay at school. Firstly, because jobs are becoming more and more specialised and technical, it is almost impossible for 16-year-olds to find work. Secondly, if they stay at school, they will receive the education and training which will create opportunities for them in the future.

§3 — Arguments AGAINST (counter-arguments)

On the other hand, many students would like to leave school at 16. This is because they find school difficult or they do not enjoy studying. For that reason, they would prefer to be working and earning money rather than pursuing their studies.

§4 — My own view + solution

A further point is that unmotivated students disrupt lessons, and this causes problems for students who do prefer to study. Therefore, after 16 they should only study technical or practical subjects that interest them.

§5 — Conclusion: summary + final opinion

In conclusion, I believe it is a mistake for people to leave soon, because they will miss opportunities which may arise in the future. More importantly, they will never find out who they might truly be in terms of fulfilling their dreams and truly thriving.

Model Report — Annotated Example propasalsample.docx

Task: You studied abroad as part of an exchange. Why did you do it? What did you like/dislike? Make recommendations.

Title + Introduction

Pegasus Educational Exchange Programme
The purpose of this report is to outline my reasons for doing the exchange, the positive and negative aspects of the experience and to make recommendations for improvements.

Section: Reasons for doing the exchange

I have been in New Zealand as part of the Pegasus programme for the last nine months. I participated in the exchange in order to do an MSc in Environmental Science. At the same time, I had the opportunity to improve my English.

Section: Positive and negative aspects

I think that the benefits of doing this exchange far outweigh the disadvantages. The main professional advantage is the opportunity to exchange ideas with teachers and students from another part of the world. As a result, I believe that I will return to my country with innovative solutions to local problems. In addition, I have acquired improved language skills.

The negative aspect from my point of view is that the programme does not receive enough financial support and, as a result, it has been very expensive. This is a problem which is likely to discourage prospective exchange students.

Section: Recommendations

I would recommend that the programme increases its financial support for students. It would also be a good idea to provide a dedicated coordinator for each exchange student during their first month abroad.

Inversion (Destination B2 – Unit 27)

Inversion with Negative / Restrictive Adverbials

When certain negative or restrictive adverbs/phrases are placed at the beginning of a sentence for emphasis, the subject and auxiliary are inverted (question word order).

Adverbial phraseNormal word orderInverted (emphatic)
NeverI have never seen such a mess.Never have I seen such a mess.
Rarely / SeldomShe rarely complains.Rarely does she complain.
Hardly … whenWe had hardly arrived when it started raining.Hardly had we arrived when it started raining.
Scarcely … whenShe had scarcely sat down when the phone rang.Scarcely had she sat down when the phone rang.
No sooner … thanHe had no sooner left than it began to snow.No sooner had he left than it began to snow.
Not untilI didn't realise until later.Not until later did I realise.
Not only … but alsoShe not only sings but also dances.Not only does she sing but she also dances.
Only thenI understood only then.Only then did I understand.
Only when / ifOnly when you leave, you will understand.Only when you leave will you understand.
Watch out! If there is no auxiliary verb, use do/does/did for inversion: Rarely does he go out.

Inversion with Possessives nor / neither / so / such

StructureExample

So + aux + subject (agreement – positive)She can swim. So can I.
Such + be + subjectSuch was her anger that she left.

Present Perfect Simple vs Past Simple

Present Perfect Simple (have / has + past participle)

UseSignal wordsExample
Finished action with present relevancejust, already, yet, recently, latelyI have just finished my homework.
Life experience (unspecified time)ever, never, before, once, twiceHave you ever been to Japan?
Action in an unfinished time periodtoday, this week, this year, so farShe has written three emails today.
Action starting in past, continuing nowfor, since, how longI have lived here for ten years.
Watch out! Use for + duration: for two days. Use since + point in time: since Monday.

Past Simple

UseSignal wordsExample
Completed action at a specific past timeyesterday, last week, in 2010, ago, when, thenShe moved to Paris in 2015.
Series of completed actions in the pastfirst, then, after that, finallyHe woke up, showered and left.
Past habit or state (no longer true)used to, would, alwaysWe used to visit every summer.
Watch out! Never use past simple with since, ever, yet, already, just (in British English).
